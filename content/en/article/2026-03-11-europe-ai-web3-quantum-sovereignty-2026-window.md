Europe is entering 2026 with a strategic paradox: there are more signals than ever of technological capability (federated infrastructure, public AI models, applied quantum advances, tokenization in sandboxes), yet there is also rising uncertainty about the industrial and regulatory framework that will determine whether these capabilities translate into prosperity at home or are externalized.

Convergence is the differentiator. EURO-3C and ‘Quantum Telco’ matter not as isolated technical novelties, but because they point to a European stack where network, edge/cloud, AI and post‑quantum security are orchestrated as strategic infrastructure. In the AI economy, whoever controls infrastructure also controls room to maneuver: cost, performance, compliance, and the ability to innovate without asking for permission.

At the same time, Web3 is moving beyond the “crypto yes/no” debate toward a redesign of financial and trust rails. The end of the PSD2–MiCA transition bridge for stablecoins (EMTs) is a maturity test: regulation starts treating tokenization and on‑chain payments as a continuum, forcing operators to professionalize (licenses, capital, controls) while rewarding those who invest early in compliance-by-design and responsible architectures.

The core question is governance and competitiveness: if a draft industrial policy can remove AI and quantum from the strategic technology list, the risk is not “lack of innovation” but “lack of decision”. The biggest threat in the manufactured‑intelligence revolution is not making mistakes, but arriving late. Spain and Europe need coherence between (1) rights‑protecting regulation that does not suffocate innovation, (2) investment in sovereign capabilities (data, compute, talent) and (3) IP and transparency frameworks that incentivize building and scaling in Europe.
