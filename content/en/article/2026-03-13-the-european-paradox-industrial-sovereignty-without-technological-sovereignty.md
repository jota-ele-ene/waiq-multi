This week presents a revealing paradox about Europe's technological direction. On one hand, a leaked draft of the Industrial Accelerator Act removes artificial intelligence, semiconductors, and quantum computing from the list of strategic technologies that would receive 'made in Europe' protection. On the other, Spain announces €100 million to boost European digital sovereignty, with companies like Telefónica, Indra, and Multiverse Computing among the projects selected for IPCEI-AI. The contradiction speaks volumes: while Brussels yields to divisions among member states and limits its technological ambition to heavy industries and clean energy, some countries are taking the initiative individually.

Meanwhile, the regulatory framework accelerates on multiple fronts. Full application of the EU AI Act arrives in August 2026, imposing exhaustive requirements for high-risk systems, deepfake transparency, and national regulatory sandboxes. Across the Atlantic, US states are legislating on AI with a pragmatic use-case approach —chatbots, healthcare, algorithmic pricing— that includes mandatory audits for frontier models. In the Web3 space, the MiCA clock keeps ticking: France's AMF warns that 30% of registered crypto firms have not communicated their plans three months before the transition period ends. The WEF's 3C framework —Combination, Convergence, Compounding— manifests clearly here: regulated technologies don't advance in silos but interweave, and normative frameworks must evolve at the same pace.

But perhaps the most significant development this week is the emergence of post-quantum cryptography (PQC) as a business agenda item rather than merely an academic concern. The ACM warns that PQC migration can take 5 to 15 years, MWC Barcelona dedicates a specific session to it, and Wavestone publishes its first commercial solutions radar. The 'harvest now, decrypt later' risk —adversaries capturing encrypted traffic today to decrypt with future quantum computers— turns what seems like a future problem into a present urgency. Companies that don't begin their cryptographic inventory now could find themselves in a 3-to-5-year vulnerability window when the first capable quantum computers arrive.

The convergence of regulation, sovereignty, and technological preparedness defines the current moment. Europe has the normative tools —AI Act, MiCA, Digital Services Act— but seems to waver in industrial ambition. The risk, as the WAIQ manifesto notes, is not in advancing too fast but in falling behind. The question European decision-makers must ask themselves is whether industrial sovereignty that excludes the technologies defining the next decade is truly sovereignty at all.
